# Deep-research prompts for building the skill pack

These prompts generate the research docs that become the pack's knowledge skills. Each one is written to produce output in the **same shape as the existing research docs** (the RPG Codex and the Open-World Bible), so the result drops cleanly into a skill's `SKILL.md` / `GUIDE.md` / `CHECKLIST.md`.

**How to use:** paste **[CONTEXT]** + **[FORMAT]** + one **[TOPIC]** block into a deep-research tool (e.g. the `/deep-research` skill), and run. One topic = one skill. Then hand the result back to Claude with "fold this into the `<skill-name>` skill."

The richer and more cited the research, the better the skill — every "rule + exemplar + test criterion + failure mode" maps directly onto a checklist item.

---

## [CONTEXT] — paste this first, every time

> You are a senior game-design researcher producing a reference document for an **AI game designer**. The consumer of your document is not a human reading for pleasure — it is an autonomous coding agent (Claude Code, running headless) that designs and generates games. Assume:
> - **Engine-agnostic, implemented in Godot.** Do not give engine-specific code; stay at the design/spec level.
> - **Procedural / AI-authored.** The game's content is generated by an AI engine from rules and constraints, with a hand-authored backbone. Bias toward techniques that survive procedural generation, and explicitly flag where a technique needs hand-authoring.
> - **Signature mechanics of the running example game ("Valenfeld"):** a 3D open-world action-RPG inspired by Morrowind, with *finite legendary loot*, *single-save high-lethality* combat, a *diegetic token economy*, and an *AI-authored procedural content engine*. Use these as recurring worked examples where relevant, but keep the principles general.
> - **The agent will act on your rules literally**, so every rule must be testable and every failure mode must be named.

## [FORMAT] — paste this second, every time

> Produce a document with these sections:
> 1. **TL;DR** — 3–5 bullets of the highest-leverage takeaways.
> 2. **Key Findings** — a numbered list of the core principles, each one sentence of claim + one of support.
> 3. **Details** — the body, organized into the sub-domains given in the TOPIC. For **every rule**, give: **(a)** the rule stated imperatively, **(b)** the exemplar game(s) that prove it, with the specific design choice and a citation/source where possible, **(c)** a **testable checklist criterion** phrased as "Test for: …" (something an agent can check against a design or a generated artifact), and **(d)** the named **failure mode / anti-pattern** to avoid.
> 4. **Recommendations** — staged, actionable steps for the AI designer, plus "thresholds that change the plan."
> 5. **Caveats** — where the evidence is thin, contested, translated, community-opinion, or where AI-authoring adds a risk the source literature doesn't cover.
>
> Requirements: cite real talks, postmortems, papers, and developer interviews (GDC, CEDEC, dev blogs, design texts) with enough detail to verify. Prefer named games and named techniques over generic advice. Always show trade-offs, not just best practices. Call out, in a dedicated note per sub-domain, **the implication for procedural / headless / AI-authored generation.**

## [TOPIC] blocks — paste one of these third

### 1 · game-design-fundamentals  *(build this first — the spine other skills reference)*
> **Topic: the foundations of game design.** Cover: (1) Sid Meier's "a series of interesting decisions" — what makes a decision interesting (believed consequences, predictability, trade-off/opportunity cost, no dominant option), and how to audit a system against it; (2) flow theory (Csikszentmihalyi) and difficulty curves — macro vs. micro flow, the challenge≈skill channel, teaching mechanics one at a time; (3) reward schedules and motivation — intrinsic vs. extrinsic, immediate vs. projected ("one more turn"), the self-determination theory lens (competence/autonomy/relatedness); (4) feedback, player agency, and the psychology of progression; (5) analysis paralysis and choice architecture. Seed sources: Sid Meier GDC 2012 "Interesting Decisions"; Jesse Schell *The Art of Game Design*; Csikszentmihalyi *Flow*; Ryan & Deci (SDT); Civilization design lore.

### 2 · combat-and-game-feel
> **Topic: combat design and game feel for a high-lethality action-RPG.** Cover: (1) game feel / "juice" (Steve Swink; Kyle Gray et al.) — the impact toolkit (hit-stop, screen shake, slow-mo, VFX, sound, hit-reactions) and the empirical caution that *too much* juice hurts (Kao); (2) readability & telegraphing — anticipation animations, consistent visual language, danger cues (Sekiro's 危), "relative damage must match perceived danger" — why this is *fairness* in a lethal game; (3) enemy & encounter design — unique silhouettes, combat roles, the "door problem" (Andrew Yoder), readable chaos, asset-efficient variety (God of War Valkyries); (4) Souls-like high-lethality combat — stamina/animation-priority, death-as-teacher, risk/reward, build viability. Seed sources: Swink *Game Feel*; *Death's Gambit* designers on telegraphing; FromSoftware combat analyses; Andrew Yoder "door problem"; Kao juiciness studies.

### 3 · rpg-systems  *(progression · economy · loot)*
> **Topic: the core RPG systems triad.** Cover three linked sub-domains: **(A) Character progression** — classes vs. classless/learning-by-doing (Morrowind lineage), the "master of everything" convergence and fixes (specialization incentives, opportunity costs), level-scaling traps (Oblivion). **(B) Economy** — the faucet/sink model, faucet-and-drain (Simpson/Ultima Online), the pinch point, stock vs. flow, and the *inverse* problem for a finite/scarcity economy (deflation/hoarding, designing compelling sinks, scarcity-as-value). **(C) Loot & itemization** — the rarity ladder, the Diablo 3 Loot 2.0 lesson (fewer/more-meaningful drops, build-defining legendary affixes), and how to design *finite legendary loot* as hand-authored set pieces with identity, lore, and placement. Seed sources: Diablo 3 Reaper of Souls loot redesign; Zachary Booth Simpson on game economies; Morrowind/Oblivion progression critiques; community loot-rarity discourse.

### 4 · systemic-emergent-design
> **Topic: the immersive-sim / systems-over-scripting philosophy.** Cover: (1) the Looking Glass / Arkane / Warren Spector lineage and the definition of "immersive sim"; (2) emergent gameplay from a small set of deep, interacting systems vs. scripted events (Mark Brown's differentiator); (3) the "tabletop RPG with a good GM" analogy — rules keep it a game, the game reacts to the player; (4) multiplicative vs. additive design (BotW chemistry engine, TotK everything-physics-driven, Takayama GDC 2024) — authoring affordances not solutions; (5) how emergent design and procedural generation multiply each other. Seed sources: Spector's Deus Ex postmortem and Ultima VI anecdote; Doug Church; Mark Brown GMT on immersive sims; Nintendo CEDEC/GDC physics talks.

### 5 · permadeath-and-lethality
> **Topic: single-save, high-lethality, and meta-progression.** Cover: (1) how permadeath converts every decision into a real consequence (the purest form of "interesting decisions") and why it demands fairness; (2) mitigations — short run length, opt-in difficulty (Hades God Mode mechanics), accessibility; (3) meta-progression — "failure is progress," diegetically-integrated death (Hades' Zagreus), roguelike vs. roguelite; (4) **the open question this skill must address head-on:** single-save + *long-form open world* is rare and under-explored — how to preserve high-lethality stakes while building diegetic, lore-justified persistence so catastrophic loss of long progress doesn't kill the game. Seed sources: Hades design (Greg Kasavin); roguelike/roguelite design discourse; permadeath player-psychology writing. **Flag heavily that this is novel territory needing playtesting.**

### 6 · worldbuilding-and-lore
> **Topic: worldbuilding and lore delivery.** Cover: (1) the iceberg principle — Hemingway (know the whole berg) vs. Sanderson (pragmatic "fake it"); building a world bible vs. revealing only what the player needs; (2) environmental & item-description storytelling, "show don't tell" (FromSoftware fragments, Morrowind's alien-but-coherent Vvardenfell, RDR2 aftermath); Henry Jenkins' four modes of environmental storytelling; (3) internal consistency and a distinct "alien but coherent" identity; (4) mythmaking and a sense of deep history (pantheons as faction proxies, N.K. Jemisin "growing your iceberg"); (5) ludonarrative harmony — mechanics that *are* the story. Seed sources: Miyazaki interviews on fragmented lore; Morrowind art-direction goals; Sanderson's worldbuilding lectures; Jenkins "Game Design as Narrative Architecture."

### 7 · narrative-and-quest-design
> **Topic: quest design, reactivity, and factions.** Cover: (1) the "no fetch quest" doctrine (CD Projekt Red / Tomaszkiewicz) — every quest needs a twist, choice, character, or consequence; the disputed Witcher-template caveat; (2) radiant/procedural quests as *supplement not substitute* (Skyrim Radiant) and how to make them respect local lore/geography; (3) deep reactivity on a budget — Sawyer's New Vegas small-scale reactivity, the "facts database" (Witcher 3, BG3's "yes-and"), the Flamethrower Principle (game completable regardless of who you kill); (4) faction design — competing goals, exclusive membership, allegiance dilemmas (New Vegas, Morrowind Great Houses). Seed sources: Tomaszkiewicz/Sasko interviews; Josh Sawyer GDC talks; Larian/BG3 reactivity interviews; New Vegas faction design.

### 8 · art-direction-and-readability
> **Topic: art direction and visual communication (design-level, not asset production).** Cover: (1) readability over fidelity — stylization ages well, photorealism is of its moment (TF2's pivot, BotW's "intentional contraction of reality"); "readability first, personality second, fidelity never"; (2) color, light, and silhouette as eye-direction and hierarchy (Elden Ring's Erdtree as gravitational beacon; reserved signal colors; alien silhouettes); (3) visual language consistency and the cost of diluting a cue; (4) **the procedural implication:** how an AI engine maintains a coherent art direction and readable signal language across generated content. Seed sources: TF2 art-direction commentary; BotW art director Satoru Takizawa; FromSoftware landmark/scale analyses; signal-color case studies (Mirror's Edge, Horizon).

### 9 · ai-authored-content-coherence
> **Topic: keeping AI-authored content coherent at scale — the crux skill for this pack.** Cover: (1) single voice via a curated corpus (Caves of Qud's 40k words) — for an LLM engine, the style/voice spec + exemplars; (2) a hard lore bible as a machine-readable constraint set with a "never violate" list (fact-checking grid to prevent retcons); (3) static backbone + procedural tissue ratio; (4) generate-then-rationalize causation and constraining references to an already-established context pool (Qud's period-by-period history); (5) recurring thematic "domains" so generated figures cohere into arcs; (6) apophenia by design; (7) **the explicit self-review pass** (compare new instances against prior ones; flag duplication) — connect this directly to the `procgen-review` skill. Seed sources: Grinblat & Bucklew FDG'17 *Subverting Historical Cause & Effect*; Kate Compton on generativity & rarity; Emily Short on procedural narrative; Dwarf Fortress emergent history. **Flag that narrative coherence at full open-world scale is unsolved — recommend de-risking with the oatmeal/fanfic tests early.**
